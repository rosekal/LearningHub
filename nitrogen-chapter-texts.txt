Atomic Identity and Periodic Classification

Nitrogen is classified as a period 2 pnictogen whose elemental form is a strongly bonded diatomic nonmetal. In periodic terms it occupies period 2, group 15, p-block, and the neutral atom is written as [He] 2s2 2p3. Those descriptors are useful because they place nitrogen inside a broader map of size, valence, and typical compound formation. Nitrogen is classified as a period-2 pnictogen, but the physical and chemical identity of elemental nitrogen is dominated by the unusual strength of the N≡N bond.

Periodic placement matters because the half-filled p subshell supports both stability in N2 and rich oxidation-state chemistry in compounds. Nitrogen occupies the nonmetallic top of group 15 and sets the pattern from which phosphorus and heavier pnictogens diverge. Its half-filled p subshell is a key reason that elemental nitrogen is both stable and electronically distinctive. When chemists compare nitrogen with nearby elements, they are comparing how similar electron counts generate recurring patterns and where size, shielding, or covalency force those patterns to bend.

One of the most instructive features of nitrogen is that nitrogen sits at the center of a group that progresses from nonmetallic molecular chemistry to metallic and semimetallic behavior. That point keeps classification from becoming mechanical. It shows why group labels are helpful starting points but not substitutes for actual chemical reasoning about structure, charge distribution, and bond type.

The classification chapter is essential because later redox chemistry of nitrogen compounds spans an unusually wide range. Physical Properties and Structural Forms

A standout feature is that the N≡N bond gives unusual thermodynamic stability to the gas. Chemical Properties and Reactivity

The reactivity profile of nitrogen is best summarized as elemental nitrogen is relatively inert, whereas many nitrogen compounds are highly reactive. That single description already implies a balance between thermodynamic driving force, kinetic accessibility, and the types of bonds the element prefers to form or break. Nitrogen teaches a classic chemistry lesson: kinetic inertness of the element does not imply low reactivity across the rest of the element’s compounds.

A defining reaction pattern is that biological or industrial fixation converts N2 into ammonia, and oxidation leads to NOx and oxyanions. Atmospheric N2 is difficult to activate, which is why fixation by biology or industry matters so much. Once fixed, nitrogen participates in acid-base chemistry, oxidation-reduction chemistry, nitration, and highly energetic decompositions. For nitrogen, reactivity is not just a matter of whether the element reacts, but of which conditions activate it and which products are energetically favored once reaction begins.

The main explanation is that breaking the N≡N bond requires significant energy or catalytic assistance.
